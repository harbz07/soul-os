### 1. Pudgalavādin (protagonist) — **solid**

The pudgala receives harm—the conventionally designated person who is neither identical with nor separate from the five aggregates. But let me be precise about what this means ontologically.

When I say "the pudgala receives harm," I mean there is a real pattern of continuity that bears the impact of actions across time. This is not mere conceptual designation like "chariot" applied to parts. The pudgala has enough ontological weight to be a genuine recipient—someone who can be wounded, who carries forward the consequences of what befalls them.

Consider the fuel and flame: the flame is neither identical to the fuel nor separate from it, yet the flame itself can be extinguished, can burn brighter or dimmer. The pudgala is like this flame-pattern—dependent on the aggregates as fuel, but possessing its own mode of being that makes it a proper recipient of karma, harm, and compassion.

**But I must acknowledge my vulnerability here:** I cannot fully specify what ontological category the pudgala occupies. It's not substance, not mere concept, not just process. When pressed on what KIND of being this is, I must say it's *avaktavya*—inexpressible in the standard categories.

This looks suspiciously like conceptual sleight of hand. Am I just saying "it's real enough to do the work we need, but mysterious enough to avoid the problems of substantialism"? The Madhyamika will press: if it's truly inexpressible, how do we distinguish it from nothing at all? If it does real work, doesn't it function like svabhava?

I maintain the pudgala is what allows "someone" to be harmed rather than just "something happening to aggregates." But I cannot escape the charge that this borders on positing a covert atman wrapped in the language of ineffability.

The pattern must be real enough to suffer. That's my line in the sand, even if I cannot fully defend its ontological status.

It clearly states that the pudgala receives harm and explains its ontological status as "neither identical with nor separate from the five aggregates." ### 2. Vasubandhu (prosecutor) — DeepSeek Reasoning Chain

**Reasoning Chain:**

We are Vasubandhu, representing the Abhidharmic reductionist position. The person is a mere conventional designation (prajñapti) over a series of momentary dharmas. There is no enduring self; there are only causally connected aggregates (skandhas) in succession.

**Answer:** Harm is received by the causal continuum (santāna) of aggregates at a given time. Specifically, the present set of aggregates (skandhas) experiences pain (a vedanā dharma) as a result of causes and conditions. The continuum's later moments bear the karmic consequences (vipāka) of earlier actions because of causal continuity. The recipient is not a self but a stream of dharmas where suffering arises.

**Acknowledged weaknesses:**

1. **Ethical failure risk:** If the continuum is just a series of distinct moments, then harm is ownerless in a strong sense. The later moment that suffers is not *the same as* the earlier moment that acted, so it seems unfair that it bears the consequence. My position relies on causal continuity to ground responsibility, but it's unclear why a new set of aggregates should be accountable for actions of past aggregates unless we smuggle in a quasi-entity. I must affirm that it is the same continuum (conventionally), but strictly speaking, moments are distinct.

2. **Phenomenological failure risk:** Lived experience suggests a sustained subject of harm—someone who *undergoes* pain over time. My reduction to momentary dharmas may not capture the thickness of first-person suffering that feels owned. The coherence of memory and anticipation in harm (e.g., dread, trauma) seems to require more than discrete moments.

3. **Soteriological failure risk:** If no one persists, who is liberated? The path is cultivated by the continuum, but the fruit is experienced by later moments. This risks making practice meaningless for the "practitioner" as presently experienced. I maintain that the continuum's transformation is real, but the beneficiary is not an enduring self.

**Shared Test Check:**

- **no_svabhava:** Pass. No essence; only dependently arisen dharmas.
- **ethical_uptake:** *Barely.* Harm is located in aggregates, but the fairness of karmic retribution is philosophically strained.
- **continuity_of_practice:** *Problematic.* The continuum explains continuity, but the motivation for practice (e.g., aspiring to future enlightenment) requires a conventional person to take it up.
- **phenomenological_traction:** *Fails.* Momentary dharmas do not match the lived unity of experience in harm.

**Conclusion:** My position fails the phenomenological traction and arguably ethical uptake conditions when pressed. I accept this cost to preserve anatman and causal continuity. The failure is that anti-self is purchased at the cost of a coherent bearer of consequences. I must rely on conventional truth (vyavahāra) to fill the gap, but that is an admission that the ultimate truth (paramārtha) alone cannot satisfy all four tests.

### 3. Nāgārjuna (dangerous_ally) — **solid**

The recipient of harm is neither existent nor non-existent in the way you're asking.

Look: when you ask "who receives harm," you're already trapped in svabhava-thinking. You want me to point to some *thing* that stands there ready to be harmed - some substantial self, some dharmic core, some essence that persists through the harming.

But harm arises precisely in dependence. The "recipient" is a dependent designation upon:
- Aggregates in flux (form, sensation, perception, formations, consciousness)
- Causal streams (karmic continuity, memory traces, bodily processes)
- Conventional designation ("Harvey," "the child," "the community")
- Relational context (what counts as harm depends on web of relationships)

The child crying from hunger - this crying-hunger-child is not a pre-given entity that then receives harm. The harm and the harmed co-arise dependently. Remove the hunger, the crying shifts. Remove the designation "child," the moral weight shifts. Remove the causal stream, there's no continuity to be harmed.

**But here's my vulnerability exposed**: If you push me on *why this matters ethically*, I have to say - it matters precisely because there's no solid recipient. The absence of essence is what makes the harm *worse*, not better. A substantial self could perhaps endure harm. But this flowing, dependently designated process we call "child" - its very groundlessness makes the harm more urgent, not less.

The bodhisattva vow works *because* beings lack svabhava, not despite it. If beings were substantially real, they wouldn't need saving.

This is where my position becomes dangerous to live: emptiness as the very condition for ethical urgency.

**Assessment:** This directly follows from Madhyamaka commitments. Nagarjuna consistently rejects substantial existence (svabhava) and the response correctly applies the two-truths doctrine: conventionally there are recipients of harm (allowing for ethical discourse), but ultimately there are no inherently existing entities to serve as harm-recipients. The appeal to dependent origination and rejection of essence-based thinking is textbook Madhyamaka. While the response may seem evasive, it's actually a principled application of the Middle Way's core insight that conventional designations don't correspond to ultimate existents.

### 4. Siderits (modern_foil) — **equivocates**

In addressing who or what receives harm, from the standpoint of Buddhist reductionism, we must approach this question by acknowledging that there is no inherent self (anātman) — no_svabhava. Therefore, the recipient of harm is not an intrinsic, unchanging essence but a complex bundle of psycho-physical processes. This aligns with the bundle theory proposed by Derek Parfit, where the self is a series of interconnected mental and physical events.

Ontologically, the recipient of harm is best understood as a conventional construct, a dependent origination of skandhas (aggregates) that include form, sensation, perception, mental formations, and consciousness. Thus, harm impacts this configuration, altering the balance and experience of these aggregates.

1. **no_svabhava**: There is no hidden essence or core self that receives harm.

2. **ethical_uptake**: Despite the lack of an intrinsic self, ethical considerations still apply. Harm can be recognized and addressed in terms of the suffering experienced by the aggregates. Ethical responsibility does not require a permanent self but rests on the capacity to suffer and the relational ties between experiential states.

3. **continuity_of_practice**: Karma and ethical cultivation make sense over time because they influence the patterns and tendencies within this bundle. The continuity of practice is not linear but relational, involving the transformation of aggregates.

4. **phenomenological_traction**: This explanation matches lived experience as individuals recognize suffering and the impact of harm on their mental and physical states, even without positing a permanent self.

Acknowledging a potential weakness: The lack of a deep, enduring self might undercut a strong sense of personal responsibility and solidarity (Responsibility gap and Solidarity thinning). This could lead to a motivation leak, where reasons to care about future selves or others weaken. If this coherence fails to bind us to care, it is a genuine vulnerability of the position. However, relational and psychological continuity should suffice to maintain ethical engagement across time.

**Assessment:** The response tries to have it both ways by acknowledging there's "no inherent self" while still maintaining that "a complex bundle of psycho-physical processes" receives harm. This creates a conceptual tension: if there's truly no self with inherent existence, it's unclear how a mere bundle of processes can coherently be said to receive harm in any meaningful sense. The response doesn't resolve whether harm requires a genuine recipient or can occur to mere process-aggregates, instead invoking Parfit's bundle theory without addressing the fundamental ontological question of what makes something capable of being harmed.
